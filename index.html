Visualizing Flower Varieties Spatially

The following diagrams show the color and source (i.e., seed or Nook Miles Ticket island) of each variety of all eight flower species in Animal Crossing: New Horizons.

A flower variety's color is determined by its genes. Most flower species have three genes, so three dimensions are required to show all possible varieties. Those three dimensions are represented here as a stack of 2D planes. The horizontal axis represents the r/R gene, the slanted axis y/Y, and the vertical axis w/W.

Roses have four genes; the fourth dimension is represented as additional stacks. For roses, the first stack shows ss varieties, the second Ss, and the third SS.

There are three possible values for each gene (for example, rr, Rr, and RR). This means each dimension is three tiles long, high, or wide.

Breeding

Click a pair of varieties (or click a single variety twice) to show the offspring that can result from breeding that pair. These possible offspring will be highlighted, and the probability of getting each variety will be shown as a fraction on the tile. If you click two different varieties, they will each be labeled "parent". If you click the same variety twice, it will be labeled "parents".

Click the brown background to clear your selections.

Experiment with different combinations to get a feel for how flower breeding works. In general, when you breed varieties that are on edges or in corners, the results tend to be more limited than when you breed varieties that are more central. Breeding the centermost variety with itself can produce any other variety. Breeding a corner variety (e.g., the topmost, rightmost, frontmost variety) with itself produces only copies of that variety.

Rose

  1. Breed a seed red with another seed red to get black (1 in 4 chance).
    • You can work on this in parallel with steps 2 through 6.
    • Any red offspring (1 in 2 chance) are seed reds. Feed them back into this step if you don't have easy access to rose seeds.
    • Breed the black offspring with each other to make more of the same (1 in 1 chance).
    • Discard any pink offspring.
  2. Breed a seed white with another seed white to get purple1 (1 in 4 chance).
    • Discard any white offspring, because some of them can't breed with seed whites to make purple.
  3. Breed the purple1 with a seed yellow to get white2 (1 in 1 chance).
  4. Breed the white2 with a purple1 to get purple1 (1 in 4 chance) and purple2 (1 in 4 chance).
    • Discard any white offspring, unless you want to figure out which ones are seed whites and which are white2.
  5. To determine the difference between purple1 and purple2, breed each purple offspring from the previous step with a seed yellow. Let each pair reproduce several times. If a pair ever produces a yellow2 (1 in 2 chance), the purple in the pair must be a purple2.
    • Breeding a purple1 with a seed yellow can only produce white2.
    • Breeding a purple2 with a seed yellow can produce either white2 (1 in 2 chance) or yellow2 (1 in 2 chance).
  6. Produce white3 with one or both of the following pairings:
    • Breed a purple2 with another purple2 to get white3 (1 in 4 chance). Feed any purple offspring back into Step 5.
    • Breed a yellow2 with another yellow2 to get white3 (1 in 4 chance). Discard any yellow offspring.
  7. Breed the white3 with a black to get red2 (1 in 1 chance).
    • You can breed the red2 with another red2 to get blue (1 in 64 chance), or continue breeding to make a better red hybrid.
  8. Breed the red2 with a white3 to get orange (1 in 8 chance). Most of the other offspring are useful in some way.
    • All purple offspring are purple2.
    • All yellow offspring are yellow2.
    • The miscellaneous red offspring can breed with each other to make blue (at least 1 in 64 chance, at most 1 in 4). Just be aware that some of the red offspring resulting from these unidentified red-red pairings will never be able to make blue, so it's best to discard them or at least keep them separate from their red parents.
    • The white offspring are either white2 or white3, but it's not easy to tell the difference. You can cross them with seed yellow, and if you ever get any white offspring (1 in 4 chance), then both the parent and the white offspring are white2.
  9. Breed the orange with a white3 to get red3 (1 in 4 chance). The yellow2, white3, and orange offspring (1 in 4 chance each) are useful, too.
  10. Breed the red3 with another red3 to get blue (1 in 4 chance). The red3 offspring (1 in 2 chance) and white3 offspring (1 in 4 chance) are useful, too.
  11. Breed the blue with a red3 to get blue (1 in 2 chance). Or isolate the blue and keep watering it until it clones itself. (I don't know which is faster.)
  12. Breed the blue with another blue to get blue (1 in 1 chance).
